The History of Web Browsers

Before web browsers existed, computers were large machines that filled entire rooms and could barely perform basic calculations. According to Mozilla, "In 1950, computers took up whole rooms and were dumber than todayâ€™s pocket calculators." By 1960, they advanced to run more complex programs, laying the foundation for modern browsing technology.

In 1990, British computer scientist Tim Berners-Lee invented the first graphical browser and web server, naming it the WorldWideWeb. This innovation introduced the concept of connected documents on a public network. Although it eventually shut down, it sparked the development of newer browsers.

The first major breakthrough came with the Mosaic browser, which brought graphics and text together on the same page. Mosaic paved the way for Netscape Navigator developed by Marc Andreessen, and in 1995, Microsoft launched Internet Explorer, triggering the browser wars of the 1990s.

During this time, companies competed to create the fastest, most feature-rich browser. Netscape influenced technologies like JavaScript, while Microsoft introduced CSS to improve website styling. Although Internet Explorer dominated for years by being bundled with Windows, legal challenges and competition eventually led to the rise of new browsers.
